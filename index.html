Alan Turing

Here's a time line of Dr.Alan Turing's life:

  • 1912: June 23: Alan Mathison Turing is born in Paddington, London, England, to Ethel Sara and Julius Mathison Turing.
  • 1926: Attends Sherborne School in Dorset, England
  • 1931: Begins studies at King's College, University of Cambridge, England.
  • 1934: Is elected a fellow of King's College, Cambridge, after being awarded a scholarship in mathematics.
  • 1935: Graduates from Cambridge with a degree in mathematics.
  • 1936: Publishes "On Computable Numbers, with an Application to the Entscheidungsproblem," which introduces the concept of a universal Turing machine and lays the foundation for modern computer science
  • 1937: Begins work on his PhD thesis at Princeton University in the United States.
  • 1938: Returns to England and begins working at the Government Code and Cypher School at Bletchley Park, where he helps break German codes, including the Enigma machine.
  • 1940: Designs the "bombe," a machine that helps crack Enigma-encrypted messages.
  • 1941: Marries Joan Clarke, a fellow codebreaker at Bletchley Park.
  • 1942: Begins work on the Colossus, the world's first programmable electronic computer, which is used to break German codes.
  • 1945: Awarded the Order of the British Empire for his wartime work.
  • 1946: Begins work on the Automatic Computing Engine (ACE) at the National Physical Laboratory in Teddington, England.
  • 1948: Joins the Mathematics Department at the University of Manchester, where he helps develop the Manchester Mark 1 computer.
  • 1950: Publishes "Computing Machinery and Intelligence," which proposes the Turing Test as a way to measure a machine's ability to exhibit intelligent behavior
  • 1952: Convicted of homosexuality, which was then a criminal offense in the UK, and sentenced to chemical castration.
  • 1954: Commits suicide by cyanide poisoning at the age of 41.
  • 2009: British Prime Minister Gordon Brown issues an official apology on behalf of the UK government for Turing's treatment.
  • 2013: Queen Elizabeth II grants Turing a posthumous pardon.

Alan Turing's contributions to computer science and cryptography were recognized after his death, and he is widely regarded as one of the most important figures in the history of computing. His work laid the foundation for the development of modern computers and artificial intelligence, and his wartime codebreaking efforts are credited with shortening the war and saving countless lives.
